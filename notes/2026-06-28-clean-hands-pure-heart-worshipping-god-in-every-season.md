## Theme
True worship of God extends beyond singing in church to encompass every aspect of life, our actions, motives, and how we respond to others, especially in seasons of change and opposition.

## Main Scriptures
- Psalm 24
- Romans 11:36
- 1 Corinthians 3:1-7
- 1 Samuel 24
- 2 Samuel 6
- 1 Timothy 2:1-4
- Colossians 1:19-22
- John 4:24

## Summary
Psalm 24 opens by declaring that the earth belongs to God, establishing that our lives are not our own but are meant to be lived for his glory. The psalmist's question, "Who may ascend the mountain of the Lord?", is answered with "the one who has clean hands and a pure heart," meaning our worship must be reflected in both our outward actions and inward motivations. David's life illustrates this: even when King Saul hunted him, David refused to harm God's anointed and instead trusted God to judge and vindicate. David also modelled uninhibited, undignified public worship when he danced before the Lord with all his might as the ark returned to Jerusalem. Ultimately, it is only through Jesus Christ that any of us can stand holy and blameless before a holy God, and we are invited to let the King of Glory into our lives.

## Key Points

1. **Our lives belong to God, not ourselves** [t=2931]
Psalm 24:1-2 declares that the earth and everything in it belongs to the Lord. The cultural message that we are self-made and in control of our own destiny is an illusion. Events like illness, job loss, or death quickly remind us how little control we actually have. Romans 11:36 answers the question of why we exist: "For from him and through him and for him are all things." We are made by God, for God's glory, and our whole lives are instruments of worship.

2. **Worship includes clean hands and a pure heart in every area of life** [t=3107]
Psalm 24:3-4 asks who may stand in God's holy place, answering: "the one who has clean hands and a pure heart." Clean hands represent our external actions and words visible to others; a pure heart represents our internal motivations and attitudes that others cannot see. Worship is therefore not limited to singing; it includes how we honour or dishonour God in every part of life.

3. **Honouring God's anointed during leadership transitions** [t=3186]
David waited 15 years between his anointing and becoming king, during which Saul sought to kill him. In 1 Samuel 24, David had the perfect opportunity to kill Saul in a cave but refused, cutting only a corner of his robe and feeling guilty even for that. David trusted God as the righteous judge and avenger rather than taking matters into his own hands. The preacher applied this directly to City Life Church's transition from senior minister Andrew Chisum to Godwin Shim: division, jealousy, and preference for a particular style are signs of spiritual immaturity (1 Corinthians 3). Mature believers recognise that God builds his church and appoints leaders in different seasons.

4. **Responding to enemies with prayer, not retaliation** [t=3961]
The cleanliness of our hands is tested by what we do or say behind people's backs when they are unaware. David honoured Saul even as his enemy. Jesus commands us to love our enemies and pray for those who persecute us (Matthew 5). Paul urges in 1 Timothy 2 that we pray for all people and all leaders, so that we may lead peaceful, godly lives. Praying for, not against, our enemies is the pure life God can bless and vindicate.

5. **Undignified, unashamed public worship** [t=4333]
As David brought the ark of the covenant back to Jerusalem (2 Samuel 6), he danced before the Lord with all his might, stripping off his royal robes and wearing priestly linen. He even sacrificed after every six steps the whole way. When Michal despised him for it, David declared he would be willing to look even more foolish. The preacher shared honestly that she had judged others' worship and had also restricted her own to protect her reputation, before God challenged her to worship for an audience of one. John 4:24 calls us to worship in spirit and in truth, not managing our comfort, but magnifying the King of Kings.

6. **Jesus makes it possible to enter God's presence** [t=4967]
Uzzah's death for touching the ark (2 Samuel 6) illustrates that God's holy presence cannot coexist with human impurity. But Colossians 1:19-22 declares that through Christ's blood God has reconciled us to himself, bringing us into his presence holy and blameless without a single fault. We are invited to let the King of Glory into our lives, not trusting in our own righteousness but in Jesus's righteousness on our behalf.

## Takeaways to Reflect On
Take time alone with God to ask him to search your heart, your actions, words, and hidden motivations, and repent of any way you have responded wrongly to leaders, spoken against others behind their backs, or elevated your own preferences above God's. In your church's season of transition, actively pray for both outgoing and incoming leaders rather than comparing or criticising. Resist the temptation to worship cautiously in order to protect your reputation; instead, pursue undignified, wholehearted praise that honours God alone. And if you have never personally invited the King of Glory into your life, know that Jesus has made a way for you to stand before a holy God forgiven, reconciled, and free.

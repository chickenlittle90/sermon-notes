## Theme
Psalms of lament give believers a healthy, faith-filled model for processing suffering by moving from honest pain through prayer and petition to renewed praise and trust in God.

## Main Scriptures
- Psalm 13 (primary text)
- Referenced: Deuteronomy (God hiding his face), 2 Corinthians 10, the Gospels (Jesus asking "What do you want me to do for you?"), Matthew 26 (Garden of Gethsemane)

## Summary
Lament psalms make up 40% of all 150 psalms, showing that God fully expects his people to go through seasons of deep suffering. Psalm 13, one of the shortest lament psalms, gives a clear three-part structure: expressing pain honestly, petitioning God for help, and choosing to trust and praise him. Moving through these three stages reorients the believer's soul even when outward circumstances have not changed. Lament is not a failure of faith but faith struggling toward understanding and hope. Believers are encouraged to read lament psalms, pray them aloud, and even write their own following this pattern.

## Key Points

1. **What a lament is and why it matters** [t=2935] A lament is a prayer of faith that honestly brings sorrow, confusion, fear, anxiety, and suffering to God without holding back. Laments comprise 40% of all 150 psalms, more than any other genre, reflecting that God recognises his people go through hard times. Some Christians, especially in Pentecostal circles, can feel like a failure when suffering and may deny what they are going through; the laments teach a healthier way to process pain.

2. **Psalm 13 and its background** [t=3239] David wrote Psalm 13 during the roughly 13 to 14 years when King Saul hunted him, having become jealous after David slew Goliath and the people praised him. The psalm is the shortest of David's lament psalms and provides a clear model: complaint (verses 1 and 2), petition (verses 3 and 4), and praise (verses 5 and 6).

3. **Section one: the pain, honest expression of distress** [t=3470] David cries "How long, O Lord?" four times, not seeking a timetable but expressing anguish. He feels God has hidden his face and abandoned him, wrestles with his thoughts, and has sorrow in his heart all day. Mike was clear that David's feelings were not reality, since God had not left him, but the psalm shows it is right and healthy to express those feelings honestly to God rather than suppress them. Just as a child runs to a parent after falling off a bike, we are invited to bring our hurt to the Father.

4. **Our feelings are not always the truth** [t=3618] Feelings are real, but they do not always reflect reality. Feeling that God is far away does not mean he is far away. New Testament believers have an upgraded theology from David's: Jesus promised "I will never leave you nor forsake you," and the Holy Spirit dwells within permanently, not coming and going based on obedience or failure. Believing our feelings over Scripture will stop us from moving forward.

5. **Section two: the petition, shifting from complaint to request** [t=4193] This is the turning point. David moves from "How long will you hide your face?" to "Look on me and answer me, Lord." He asks God to give light to his eyes, a Hebrew expression for renewed energy and strength. Mike observed that many people get stuck in perpetual complaining to friends and pastors but never actually petition God, and it is this step of turning to ask the Father for help that begins to reorient the soul.

6. **Section three: the praise, a choice to trust** [t=4531] David ends with "I trust in your unfailing love. My heart rejoices in your salvation. I will sing to the Lord, for he has been good to me." Nothing in his circumstances changed while he was writing this psalm. Saul did not stand down. But something changed within David as he prayed. The psalm ends not with changed circumstances but with changed perspective, renewed hope, and lifted spirits. Trust is a decision, not a feeling. Mike also pointed to Jesus in Gethsemane as a perfect example of lament: expressing pain, petitioning the Father to remove the cup, then choosing "not my will but yours."

7. **Practical application: write your own psalm of lament** [t=4829] Mike encouraged people to read lament psalms aloud, believe God has not abandoned them, and write their own psalm following the three-part structure. First, write out the pain as a prayer, describing the situation and how it is affecting you. Second, write a petition, asking God specifically for what you need. Third, write out praise, recalling his faithfulness, past answered prayers, and his goodness. He closed by praying four written lament prayers on behalf of those facing illness, broken relationships, persecution, and financial hardship.

## Takeaways to Reflect On
When you are in a difficult season, bring your pain honestly to God rather than denying it or staying stuck in complaint. Read Psalm 13 and notice how David moves from raw anguish to petition to praise without any change in his circumstances. Try writing your own psalm following that three-part pattern: name your pain as a prayer, ask God specifically for what you need, and then write out reasons to trust and praise him based on his past faithfulness. Remember that feelings of God's absence are not the same as reality, and that his promise in the New Testament is that he will never leave or forsake you. Lament is not a loss of faith; it is faith wrestling its way toward hope and trust.

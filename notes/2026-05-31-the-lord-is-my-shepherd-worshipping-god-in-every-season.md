## Theme
Psalm 23 is a psalm of trust for every season of life, showing that God as shepherd provides, guides, and pursues us whether we are in times of abundance or times of hardship.

## Main Scriptures
- Psalm 23
- Psalm 22
- Psalm 24
- John 10 (Jesus as the Good Shepherd)

## Summary
Andrew Chisholm opens a new sermon series on the Psalms of David by exploring Psalm 23 as a psalm of trust written against the backdrop of David's early life as a shepherd boy. Using the two main seasons of Israel (winter rains and summer heat) as a framework, he shows how the psalm maps onto the pastoral rhythms of a shepherd's year, from green pastures and still waters in the rainy season to dangerous valleys and highland tablelands in the dry season. Throughout every season, he argues, God provides, guides, and actively pursues his people. He draws on the sequence of Psalms 22, 23, and 24 to show how lament, trust, and praise form a natural movement in worship, one that Jesus himself modelled on the cross. The sermon closes with practical suggestions for finding a voice to worship God in every season, and a crafted prayer drawn directly from the psalm.

## Key Points

1. **The Psalms of David give us a voice for every season** [t=2938]: There are 73 psalms attributed to David in one way or another, covering genres including trust, lament, praise, repentance, and wisdom. David worshipped God through his entire life, from shepherd boy to fallen king to aged king, and his psalms provide a template for us to do the same.

2. **Understanding the two seasons of Israel unlocks Psalm 23** [t=3544]: Israel has fundamentally two seasons, not four. Winter brought rain, new pasture, and relative abundance. Summer brought intense heat, dried pasture, danger, and scarcity. Reading Psalm 23 against this background, the opening verses (green pastures, still waters, restored soul) picture the rainy season, while the later verses (dark valleys, the prepared table, anointing with oil, the overflowing cup) picture the stressful summer season when the shepherd guides his flock through danger to highland pastures.

3. **"Goodness and mercy shall follow me" means God actively pursues us** [t=4032]: The Hebrew word translated "follow" carries the sense of chasing after or running after. Andrew illustrated this with the image of a shepherd sprinting toward a sheep attacked by a lion, or running to rescue a lamb that has fallen into a bush. Eugene Peterson's paraphrase in The Message captures this: "Your beauty and love chase after me every day of my life." This points forward to Jesus, the Good Shepherd, who leaves the 99 to pursue the one lost sheep.

4. **Jesus modelled worshipping God in every season through the Psalms** [t=4205]: On the cross, Jesus prayed Psalm 22, a psalm of lament that begins "My God, my God, why have you forsaken me?" but moves towards praise by its end. Jesus knew that Psalm 22 was followed by Psalm 23 (trust) and then Psalm 24 (praise of the King of kings). By trusting God in the darkest moment, he knew resurrection life was coming. This sequence of lament, trust, and praise is a pattern we can follow in our own seasons.

5. **Practical ways to worship God in every season** [t=4418]: Andrew offered several suggestions. First, dive into the Psalms and find the psalm that matches your season, then pray it out loud or in your own words. Second, journal on the Psalms, writing down what you sense God speaking to you. Third, memorise key psalms so they are available in any moment, on a run, in a waiting room, or in the night. Fourth, build a personal worship playlist of songs that have ministered to you in past seasons, because those songs can draw you back into an awareness of God's presence. He closed with the story of an actor and an old pastor both reciting Psalm 23: the actor knew the psalm brilliantly, but the pastor knew the Shepherd personally, and the room was moved to tears. The point was that all the practices are simply tools to help us know the Shepherd himself.

## Takeaways to Reflect On
Consider which season you are currently in, whether a time of green pastures and refreshing or a time of summer heat and pressure, and find a psalm that gives voice to that experience. Read the whole psalm rather than stopping at one verse, and notice how it moves. Try recrafting a psalm in your own words as a personal prayer. Begin memorising even one or two psalms so they are with you at all times. Build a playlist of songs that have previously drawn you close to God and return to them intentionally. Above all, the goal is not simply to know about God or to know the right words, but to know the Shepherd personally and to trust him through every season of life.

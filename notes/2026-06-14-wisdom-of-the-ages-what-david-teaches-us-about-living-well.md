## Theme
Drawing on five wisdom Psalms of David, one from each book of the Psalter, the sermon explores what a lifetime of walking with God teaches us about how to live well, trust God through all circumstances, and pass that faith on to others.

## Main Scriptures
Psalm 37, Psalm 71, Psalm 86, Psalm 103, Psalm 145

## Summary
The sermon opens by noting that people at the end of their lives most often regret not investing in relationships, not living courageously, and not allowing themselves to be happy. True biblical wisdom, the preacher argues, is accumulated over a lifetime of seeking God with an undivided heart, not just gathered in individual moments. Working through one Psalm of David from each of the five books of the Psalter, the sermon shows that David consistently acknowledges the hardships of life while returning again and again to the unchanging character of God as the foundation for hope. This accumulated, experienced wisdom must then be passed on to the next generation. The sermon closes by tracing the Psalter's overarching storyline, which points beyond David to a coming Messiah, fulfilled in Jesus Christ, who is returning and whose kingdom is everlasting.

## Key Points

1. **Wisdom starts with a lifetime of seeking God, not just moments** [t=3186]
   The sermon begins with a question: what do people regret at the end of life? Research from palliative care workers points to three recurring regrets: not investing in relationships, not living courageously, and not enjoying life. The preacher introduces Wayne Cordeiro's observation that wisdom can come from our own pain and mistakes, from the lessons of others, or from the guidance of God. Biblical wisdom literature (Proverbs, Ecclesiastes, Job) deals with the dilemmas of injustice, pain, and brokenness and always concludes that true wisdom begins with the fear of the Lord.

2. **Psalm 37: Do not envy the wicked. God will deliver justice in the end** [t=3467]
   David writes from old age ("I was young and now I am old") and contrasts the fate of the wicked with those who hope in the Lord. He does not ignore injustice but says it will not last. Those who trust, delight, commit, are still, and hope in the Lord will be blessed and vindicated. For those who have experienced unfairness, the promise that God will ultimately deliver justice is not harsh but comforting.

3. **Psalm 71: God is unchanging from birth to old age, and that is the ground of confident hope** [t=3765]
   David says God has been his hope since youth, and he pleads that God will not abandon him in old age. He names troubles as "many and bitter" but declares unwavering hope because God is a rock and fortress. This is not vague or wishful hope but a confident, earned certainty. David's response is to declare God's deeds to the next generation so that his life-changing confidence can benefit others.

4. **Psalm 86: Circumstances change but God's character never does** [t=4093]
   David cries out in lament while enemies threaten his life, yet he pivots to focus on who God is rather than what his circumstances look like. The preacher warns against interpreting God through our circumstances, since circumstances always shift. Because God is compassionate, gracious, slow to anger, and abounding in love and faithfulness, he can be fully trusted at all times. The right response is an undivided heart given wholly to him.

5. **Psalm 103: God's everlasting love calls us to pass faith to the next generation** [t=4413]
   David praises God not only for what he does but for who he is. He uses the image of a father's compassion, and notes that human life is as brief as grass. Yet from everlasting to everlasting God's love rests on those who fear him, and his righteousness extends to their children's children. The question becomes: how do we pass this wisdom, blessing, and knowledge of God on to those who come after us?

6. **Psalm 145: David's final Psalm commits to praising God every day, and points to an everlasting kingdom** [t=4633]
   As the last Psalm of David in the entire Psalter, it is a declaration that he will praise God forever and ever, every single day. One generation commends God's works to the next. The preacher then unpacks why the Psalter has five books: Book 1 centres on David as king; Book 2 affirms the Davidic promise continues into Solomon; Book 3 voices the anguish of exile when the throne is gone; Book 4 points back to Moses to remind Israel that God was faithful before David ever existed; and Book 5 calls the people to hope not in David but in God himself, and in the coming Messiah. That hope was fulfilled in Jesus, who sits on David's throne forever and is coming back.

## Takeaways to Reflect On
Wisdom is not built overnight; it grows through years of consistently bringing everything, the good and the painful, honestly before God. When life feels unfair, resist the temptation to read God's character through your circumstances, since he is always compassionate, gracious, and faithful even when situations are not. Cultivate an undivided heart by trusting, delighting in, and committing your way to God every day. Consider seriously who you are passing your faith on to, whether children, grandchildren, or anyone in the next generation, because to hoard something so good is contrary to wisdom itself. Finally, live in active anticipation of the return of Jesus, the king whose everlasting kingdom gives real and confident hope in every season of life.

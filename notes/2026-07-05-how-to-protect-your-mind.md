## Theme
The sermon calls Christians to actively guard their minds by controlling what they allow in through their eyes, ears, and relationships, and by filling their thoughts with God's word and prayer.

## Main Scriptures
- Proverbs 4:23
- Proverbs 23:7
- Psalm 101:3
- Romans 10:17
- 1 Corinthians 15:33
- Philippians 4:6-8
- 1 Samuel 30:6

## Summary
Praba opens by drawing a parallel between national border security and the need to guard our minds, noting that whatever we fail to protect we will eventually lose. He identifies three key "gates" through which harmful thoughts enter: the eye gate, the ear gate, and the influence gate. Using Philippians 4:6-8, he gives practical steps for protecting the mind, centred on prayer, peace, and intentional thinking. He draws on David's example in 1 Samuel 30, where David encouraged himself in God despite devastating loss, to show that guarding the mind means choosing where to run when life falls apart. The sermon closes with a call to replace the enemy's lies with the declared promises of God.

## Key Points

1. **Whatever you fail to protect, you will eventually lose** [t=3452] Just as governments spend millions on border security to keep harmful things out of the country, the Bible calls us to guard our minds. Proverbs 4:23 says to guard the mind above all else because life flows from it. The greatest battle we face is not financial, relational, or physical; it is the battle for our minds, because if the enemy can influence our thinking he can influence the direction of our lives.

2. **The enemy's strategy begins with a thought** [t=3580] In Genesis, Satan did not start with violence but with one question: "Did God really say?" Every temptation begins with a thought. The first Adam believed the enemy's lies; Jesus, the second Adam, defeated them with the word of God in the wilderness. Not positive thinking, but the word of God, is our weapon.

3. **Guard the eye gate** [t=3751] Psalm 101:3 shows that David chose not to set worthless things before his eyes. We live in a world constantly fighting for our attention, from social media to news channels. The question is not whether technology is good or bad, but whether what we watch draws us closer to God or pushes us away.

4. **Guard the ear gate** [t=3920] Romans 10:17 says faith comes from hearing, which means fear can also grow by hearing. Every voice we listen to, whether a podcast, music, or conversation, shapes the way we think and can become our inner voice. We must ensure the loudest voice in our lives remains the voice of God.

5. **Guard the influence gate** [t=4027] 1 Corinthians 15:33 warns that bad company corrupts good character. Praba shared that he intentionally limits time with people who leave him discouraged and seeks out those who build his faith. The people around us are not just influencing our behaviour; they are shaping our thinking.

6. **Pray rather than rehearse your problems** [t=4199] Philippians 4:6-7 instructs us not to be anxious about anything but to bring everything to God in prayer with thanksgiving. Paul wrote this from prison, yet spoke of peace and joy. He does not tell us to try harder or pretend everything is fine; he says pray, and God's peace, which surpasses understanding, will guard our hearts and minds. The world's peace depends on circumstances; God's peace depends on who is with us.

7. **Choose which thoughts you allow to stay** [t=4454] Philippians 4:8 gives a filter: whatever is true, noble, right, pure, lovely, and admirable, think on these things. We cannot stop every negative thought from entering our mind, just as we cannot stop birds flying overhead, but we can stop them from building a nest. The practical response is to name the thought, reject it, and replace it with the word of God, declaring specific scriptures against fear, anxiety, lack, and loneliness.

8. **Encourage yourself in the Lord** [t=4621] In 1 Samuel 30, David returned to Ziklag to find the city burned, his family taken, and his own men speaking of stoning him. Yet the Bible says David encouraged himself in the Lord. He could not change his circumstances, but he chose where to run. The greatest sermon we may ever hear is the one we preach to ourselves. Praba shared that a close friend from his childhood in Sri Lanka believed hurtful words spoken over him, and the outcome was tragic, a reminder that what enters our minds carries real power.

## Takeaways to Reflect On
Be intentional about what you watch, what you listen to, and who you allow to speak into your life, because each of these gates shapes your thinking. When anxiety or fear arises, resist the urge to rehearse the problem and instead bring it to God in prayer. Prepare a few specific scripture declarations in advance so that when negative thoughts come you can name them, reject them, and replace them with God's word. And in moments when no one is there to encourage you, follow David's example: encourage yourself in the Lord, speaking his promises over your family, your health, your finances, and your future.

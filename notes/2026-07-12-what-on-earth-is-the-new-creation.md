## Theme
The sermon explains what it means to be a "new creation" in Christ, rooting personal Christian identity in the overlap of two ages in God's redemptive history.

## Main Scriptures
- 2 Corinthians 5:14-17
- Galatians 1:4
- Romans 5:12-21
- John 17 (high priestly prayer)
- Ephesians 4

## Summary
Paul's declaration in 2 Corinthians 5:17 that anyone "in Christ" is a new creation is not primarily about changed personal feelings or appetites but about a genuine change of status and existence. Human history is divided into two overlapping ages: the present evil age, marked by Adam's sin, condemnation, and death, and the age to come, inaugurated by Jesus at his first coming and to be completed at his return. Believers now live in the overlap of these two ages, having been transferred out of Adam and into Christ, which is an ontological transformation of identity, status, and purpose. Everything has changed except the physical body, which will be redeemed at the resurrection. Because of this, Christians are called to live out what they already are, not to strive to become something they hope one day to achieve.

## Key Points

1. **The popular interpretation of 2 Corinthians 5:17 is incomplete** [t=3538] Many people read "the old has passed away" as referring to old sinful desires being replaced by new spiritual ones. Mike argues this is not Paul's point. The verse is about a change of realm and status, not primarily a change in felt experience.

2. **Human history is divided into two ages** [t=3578] Drawing on a diagram he prepared, Mike explained that history runs from creation through to the end of time. Jesus' first coming inaugurated "the age to come," bringing the future into the present. Believers now live in the escaton, the overlap between the present evil age and the age to come. The kingdom has already come but is not yet fully consummated.

3. **Being "in Adam" versus being "in Christ"** [t=3815] Because all humanity was genetically and representatively inside Adam when he sinned, every person is born into sin, condemnation, and death. When a person trusts in Jesus, God no longer views them as being in Adam. They are now in Christ, a union with Jesus described as a new creation. This is not a minor adjustment but a complete change of race and realm.

4. **The new creation is an ontological transformation** [t=4512] Mike used Jesus' words in John 17, "They are not of this world, even as I am not of this world," to show how radical this is. Just as Jesus was not originally from this world, believers are no longer considered by God to belong to the old creation. Everything has changed: status, relationship with God, purpose, and future. The only exception is the physical body, whose redemption awaits the resurrection.

5. **The indicative and imperative: be what you are** [t=4768] New Testament scholars distinguish between indicative statements (facts about who we are, for example, "you are holy") and imperatives (commands, for example, "live a holy life"). Many Christians try to live holy in order to become holy. Mike argues it works the other way: we have been made holy in Christ, therefore we live accordingly. Putting off the old man and putting on the new man, as Ephesians 4 describes, means living like what we have already become.

6. **Practical stewardship as new creation people** [t=4876] Because we are new creations, we are stewards of what God has given us: the gospel and the ministry of reconciliation, spiritual gifts for serving God and others, and financial resources. Mike noted that roughly one quarter of Jesus' parables concern financial stewardship. Faithful participation in God's redemptive plan, including sharing the gospel, using gifts, and giving, flows from understanding our identity.

7. **Pray and live as children of the royal family** [t=5090] Mike recounted visiting Buckingham Palace and being refused entry, illustrating that proximity to royalty does not make you part of it. You must be born in or adopted. The New Testament teaches both: believers are born again and adopted as children of God. His encouragement was to pray to God as "Father," even "Abba," the intimate word Jesus used in Gethsemane, because as new creation people we are no longer distant from God but genuinely his children.

## Takeaways to Reflect On
Consider that your identity in Christ is already complete, not something to be earned through effort or good behaviour. When you sin and confess, your status before God does not change. Try reading Scripture with the awareness that you are part of God's redemptive plan in history, finding yourself in its pages as a new creation person. Make a habit of addressing God as Father in prayer, and allow that intimacy to shape how you walk through each day. Use your gifts, your resources, and your voice to share the gospel, living as a steward of the grace you have freely received.
